# Executive Communication

## Source
*Executive Communication & Influence* workshop by Wes Kao. Core topic: making your ideas feel obvious to others by framing around your audience, sharing your reasoning, and reducing friction.

## Overview
Every interaction shapes how people perceive Tristan's judgment. The gap between good and great operators is rarely strategy — it's the ability to make solutions feel obvious to others. 80% of communication breakdowns stem from three problems: not framing around the recipient, unexplained logic, and excessive cognitive load. Strong communication compounds — every Slack message, email, and meeting shapes coworkers' entire understanding of working with Tristan.

## Framework

### 1. RLC Foundation
**What it means:** Three root causes drive most communication breakdowns. RLC tackles all three.

- **Recipient-focused (90/10 rule):** Frame 90% around the other person's goals, priorities, and concerns. 10% about Tristan. Ask "who is this person and what might they find compelling?" before sending anything. Appeal to their function, role, or organizational goals. De-risk their hesitations proactively.
- **Share your Logic:** Add "because" after every recommendation. Logic means rationale, thought process, data, assumptions, evidence, examples, first principles — not just hard numbers. Use minimum effective dose: controversial claims need more logic, routine decisions need less. Without seeing reasoning, your audience can't evaluate your ideas or build trust in your judgment.
- **Reduce Cognitive load:** Main point first, context below. Complete sentences (fragments save writer time but cost reader time). State times in recipient's timezone. Be explicit about what you need (FYI vs. action needed). Avoid pronoun soup (replace "this/that/it" with specific nouns). End on your question — don't bury it. Summarize before adding someone to a long thread.

**Diagnostic trigger:** Tristan is drafting any written communication — Slack message, email, doc, proposal, update. Or preparing talking points for a meeting.

**Application:**
- Read the draft through the recipient's eyes. What do they care about? What feels risky for them? What would make them feel safe saying yes?
- Check: is the main point in the first line, or buried after backstory?
- Check: does every recommendation have a "because"?
- Use signposting keywords to guide the reader: "First / Second / Finally" (hierarchy), "For example" (evidence), "Because" (logic), "As a next step" (executives zoom in on this), "To be clear" (prevents misreading), "Up until now / going forward" (time boundary).
- Bold only key phrases — if 30% is bold, nothing stands out. Rough ceiling: 10%.
- Pattern to follow: "I recommend [action] because [reason]. The potential risks are [X], but we can address them by [Y]. If you agree, I can start by [first step]."

### 2. Always Be Selling
**What it means:** Every interaction is persuasion, not information transfer. Communication has two components: sales (why this matters, why they should care) and logistics (how to do it). Always lead with sales. When people don't take action, the instinct is to add more logistics — but they usually need more sales.

Explain stakes using "bad things / good things": what bad things might happen if we don't do this? What good things do we gain if we do? This can be one line each for brief messages or full sections for strategy docs.

Never frame legitimate business requests as favors. Don't turn molehills into mountains — people take your lead on whether something is a big deal.

**Diagnostic trigger:** Tristan is pitching an idea, making a proposal, asking for resources or approval, or writing anything where the audience needs to be motivated to act. Also when a request keeps getting ignored — likely a sales problem, not a logistics problem.

**Application:**
- Identify: is this draft leading with sales or logistics? If the first paragraph explains how to do something before explaining why it matters, restructure.
- Add stakes. Ask: "What happens if we don't do this? What do we gain if we do?" Even one sentence of stakes transforms a flat request into a compelling one.
- Check for favor-framing: phrases like "I hate to ask" or "sorry to bother you" signal molehill-to-mountain thinking. Replace with the business reason the request matters.
- Pattern — nursing home pitch transformation:
  - Weak: "We would love to visit... we're learning public speaking..."
  - Strong: "I'm reaching out with an idea that could bring some joy to your residents..."
  - The first line immediately shows recipient benefit.
- Pattern — the Black Friday sale pitch: lead with data on why the obvious choice (run a sale) actually hurts ("last sale cohort retention dropped 10 points"), project the consequence forward ("puts us in a worse position next year"), then offer an alternative that achieves the same goal differently. Never leave a void — if you argue against something, propose what to do instead.

### 3. Start Before the Bear
**What it means:** Cut backstory ruthlessly. Start your story at the moment of actual relevance — right before the bear shows up at camp. Don't recite the three-month chronology of booking the venue, buying equipment, and driving to the campsite.

Match altitude to your audience. Too high = vague and hand-wavy. Too low = lost in weeds. Focus 80% on what and why, 20% on how. Peers on the project can handle low altitude. Executives need high altitude. The issue is usually wrong details, not too many details — details themselves are neutral. Presenting with discernment about which details matter shows judgment.

**Diagnostic trigger:** Tristan is writing an update, answering a question, giving a status report, or presenting to someone more senior. Also when feedback sounds like "can you give me the headline?" or "what's the bottom line?"

**Application:**
- Find the bear in the draft. Where does the actual point start? Delete everything before it.
- Apply the 2-3 sentence rule for answering questions: give your answer in 2-3 sentences, then offer to elaborate on specific areas. "I can elaborate on a few areas, including [A] and [B]. What would be most helpful?" This directs the conversation while giving the audience agency.
- Check altitude. Ask: how much context does this person have? An executive who sees this project quarterly needs high altitude (results and impact). A peer who works on it daily can handle low altitude (implementation details).
- Avoid play-by-play chronological recaps. Prioritize information by importance, not by when it happened.
- Pattern — bear in camp:
  - Bad: "Three months ago we tried to book the venue but the website was glitchy, then we had to go to the store but they were sold out, then we finally got the equipment, and then Steve left food out..."
  - Good: "Steve left a protein bar out overnight and a bear came into camp. No one was hurt, but we learned to use bear canisters for all food going forward."

### 4. Don't Incept — Address
**What it means:** Two different moves that look similar but have opposite effects:

- **Incepting** = planting doubts that weren't there. Subconscious. Doesn't advance the conversation. Nixon saying "I am not a crook" gave everyone the word "crook." Before, people thought he was sketchy but lacked the vocabulary.
- **Addressing objections** = acknowledging valid concerns your audience is already thinking. Intentional. Advances the conversation by de-risking.

Ideas are fuzzy until you put them into words. Once you give people negative vocabulary, they'll use it against you.

**Diagnostic trigger:** Tristan is writing a pitch, proposal, or recommendation where the audience might have reservations. Also useful when reviewing any draft for self-sabotaging language.

**Application:**
- Scan the draft for incepting language. Common culprits:
  - "Let me know if you have concerns" → swap to "Let me know if you have questions or suggestions"
  - "I'm not trying to make your life difficult" → delete entirely
  - "I hope you'll forgive this direct approach" → delete entirely
  - Unnecessary caveats and apologies that plant doubt
- Identify the most obvious objection the audience IS already thinking. Address it directly: "I know this might seem [risky/surprising/counterintuitive] because [acknowledge their concern]. Here's why I still recommend it: [logic]."
- The test: is this concern something my audience would independently raise? If yes, address it. If not, don't introduce it.
- Pattern — Black Friday sale objection handling: "I know this might come as a surprise" (acknowledges the obvious objection that the CEO wanted a sale) followed by data showing why it hurts long-term, then an alternative that achieves the same goal. Addresses the real concern without planting new ones.

### 5. Share Your Point of View
**What it means:** Move from information delivery to recommendation. "Here are three options, what should we do?" becomes "Here are three options — I recommend B because [reasoning]. Do you agree?" Sharing your POV reduces cognitive load for your audience and positions Tristan as a strategic thinker actively contributing.

Share early and often. Avoid grand reveals — the more complex the problem, the more variables exist, and each assumption you build on can be wrong. Stacking assumptions for weeks before checking alignment is expensive when misalignment surfaces.

Use "if I don't hear from you by [date], I'll go ahead and [action]" to reduce friction and demonstrate autonomous judgment.

Distinguish between discovery writing and summarizing writing. Summarizing (updates, recaps) requires prioritizing what matters. Discovery (strategy docs, proposals) requires thinking — don't jump to a template before developing conviction. Templates used too early create "graveyards of docs with great structure but terrible content."

**Diagnostic trigger:** Tristan is presenting options to a stakeholder, writing a strategy or decision doc, sharing work for feedback, or starting a complex written deliverable. Also when tempted to wait until something is "perfect" before sharing.

**Application:**
- Check: does this draft present options without a recommendation? If so, add one with reasoning. Even "my initial hunch is leaning toward X" is better than silence.
- For complex work, shorten the feedback loop. Three days in: "Here's my initial thinking. I'm operating under [assumptions]. Right now I'm leaning toward [direction]. I'd love your input on [specific question]."
- For discovery writing: debate with yourself before using a template. Ask: Is this true? How might this not be true? What are the trade-offs? Build conviction, then organize.
- Invite dissent to show openness: "Would love to hear if you see any gaps or have suggestions." This frames feedback as collaborative, not threatening.
- Match conviction language to actual confidence level: "initial hunch" vs. "strong recommendation." Being overly confident (stating hypotheses as facts) and not confident enough (diminishing what you say with "you can ignore this") are equally damaging.

### 6. Build a Predictive Model
**What it means:** Historical analysis is backward-looking. A predictive model looks forward — anticipating how your audience will react so you can shape the interaction before it happens. Watch revealed preferences (behavior) over stated preferences (words). Your manager might say they value X, but their behavior shows they consistently prioritize Y.

Prepare for meetings even when you're not leading them. Before every meeting: (a) what is my goal, (b) what action do I need from my audience, (c) my opening 2-3 sentences spoken out loud. Even 60 seconds of prep dramatically outperforms zero prep.

Seek the question behind the question. When someone keeps asking follow-ups after you've answered thoroughly, they have a deeper concern. Signs: dissatisfaction despite good answers, repeated probing. Identify what feels risky for them and what would make them feel safe. Validate: "It sounds like you're asking about X — is that right?"

**Diagnostic trigger:** Tristan is preparing for a meeting, anticipating a stakeholder's reaction, or puzzled by why someone keeps pushing back on something that seems settled.

**Application:**
- Before a meeting, write down: (1) my goal, (2) the action I need, (3) anticipated questions from this specific person based on their patterns. Have supporting docs in open tabs.
- Frame meetings upfront: "We have [X minutes]. The goal today is [objective]. I'll start with [A], then [B]. Feel free to jump in if I'm covering ground you already know. Is there anything else you want to make sure we cover?"
- Build a running mental model of each key stakeholder: What do they always ask about? Where do they get skeptical vs. excited? What function do they represent, and what does that function care about?
- When caught off guard: "Interesting question. I'd want to think about this more, but my initial reaction is [answer]. What do you think?" Buy time without losing credibility.
- QBQ pattern — "How's that initiative going?" usually isn't small talk. The question behind it: "Is this on track? Should I be worried? Are you on top of it?" Answer the real question: "Tracking to forecast. I'm monitoring twice a week and will flag if anything changes."

### 7. New Information, Not Louder
**What it means:** When facing pushback, repeating the same argument with more passion doesn't work — it makes people dig in. New information gives your recipient a reason to reconsider without losing face. This isn't about overcoming all resistance — first ask honestly whether the pushback is valid. Sometimes they're right.

Three moves when pushback happens:
1. Share new data, evidence, or examples you haven't mentioned yet
2. Frame existing information in a new way
3. Argue their point of view for them — show you deeply understand before presenting your alternative. This stops arguments cold because they can't fall back on "you don't understand."

**Diagnostic trigger:** Tristan has made a recommendation and received pushback. Someone is unconvinced after hearing the initial pitch. A decision keeps getting revisited.

**Application:**
- First: evaluate honestly. Is this pushback valid? "I can want you to express this better AND still recognize that your core point is extremely valid." If they're right, say so and adjust.
- If your position holds, introduce new information — not the same information repeated louder. What data, example, or framing haven't you shared yet?
- Argue their side first: "I understand the concern about [X] — in fact, [articulate their argument even better than they did]. Here's what shifted my thinking: [new information]." This builds trust and creates an opening.
- Use neutral bridges: "That's definitely something we can consider. From a [function] perspective, changing X would involve Y, which would likely have [impact]. This might be worth it given the upside. With the original approach, we're able to [accomplish goal] without that downside."
- Pattern — feature name pushback:
  - CRO: "This feature name seems weak."
  - You: "We originally thought the same. This name performed well with users during testing."
  - CRO: "Financial buyers or end users?"
  - You: "Both rated it highly."
  - New information (test results with the audience the CRO cares about) resolves it cleanly.

## Tactical Toolkit

Quick-reference techniques that serve multiple principles:

**Strategic "but" placement:** Put negative information first, positive after "but" — people remember what follows "but." "This is quite far from what we're looking for, BUT this is a great learning opportunity and we started early, so we have time to improve it."

**Robot voice method:** Read your writing in monotone before sending. Anything that sounds harsh, edit. Your audience reads in their voice, not yours — you won't be there to deliver it warmly.

**Emotional signposting:** Tell people how to feel about information before delivering it. Facts aren't self-interpreting. "I wanted to share some exciting updates" vs. "I have some news" — same content, completely different experience for the recipient. Don't create emotional cliffhangers.

**The one-second-longer technique:** In group meetings, when you and someone else start talking simultaneously, continue speaking for one additional second. Usually the other person stops. Prevents always yielding the floor.

**Simplify first:** Don't write 500 words then trim to 50. Start with the 50-word constraint in mind. Understanding constraints before writing produces fundamentally different (better) output.

**Be explicit about what you need:** Replace "Can you take a look?" with specifics: what kind of feedback, by when, link to the doc. Batch requests with relative priority indicated. The "beautiful Slack message" pattern: items grouped by deadline, each with a hyperlink, priority indicated.

**Tone calibration:** Match your recipient's warmth level. If they're friendly, don't be colder. In text, turn up enthusiasm a few notches — "sounds great" not "okay." One exclamation mark per message is balanced; zero is cold, many is junior.

**Managing up escalation pattern:** When you anticipate someone will go over your head, brief your manager first with your reasoning. Align as a united front before it happens.

## Walkthrough Protocol

When this skill is invoked:
1. Ask Tristan to share the draft, situation, or problem
2. Read the material and diagnose which 2-3 principles are most relevant based on diagnostic triggers
3. For each relevant principle:
   - Explain why it applies to this specific situation
   - Walk through the application guidance with concrete recommendations
   - Reference relevant tactical toolkit items
   - Ask Tristan what to act on before moving to the next principle
4. After walking through all relevant principles, ask if Tristan wants to explore any others from the framework
